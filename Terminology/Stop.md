# Stop

In film photography, a "stop" is a unit of measurement to quantify exposure. It represents either a doubling or halving of the amount of light reaching the film. Stopping up by one unit is double the light, stopping down by one unit is halving the light.

ISO example:
- Ilford HP5+ 400, shot at 800. This is one stop of underexposure. Double it to 1600 and you're at two stops of underexposure.
- Ilford HP5+ 400, shot at 200. This is one stop of overexposure. Halve it to 100 and you're at two stops of overexposure.

Lens example:
- Lens set to an aperture of f/5.6. Bringing it to f/4 would be stopping up by one. Or bringing it up from f/5.6 to f/8 would be stopping down by one. Though, remember that adjusting aperture also affects the depth of field.
- The standard full-stop aperture sequence is:
  - f/1.0
  - f/1.4
  - f/2
  - f/2.8
  - f/4
  - f/5.6
  - f/8
  - f/11
  - f/16
  - f/22

Shutter speed example:
- When shooting at 1/250s, if you move to 1/500s, you're stopping down by one unit. Though, remember that adjusting shutter speed also affects the blur/freezing of your subject. If you move down from 1/250s to 1/125s, you're stopping up by one unit.
