# Exposure vs Development

The main thing to remember is:
- **Exposure** (what happens in the camera)
- **Development** (what happens in the darkroom)

Exposure and development are independent variables, each will do what it's meant to do, no matter what the other does.

When you're shooting film, you can choose to:
- Expose normally
- Overexpose
- Underexpose

Then, in the development process, you can choose to:
- Develop normally (datasheet development process)
- Push (increase development)
- Pull (decrease development)

You can mix and match these to get your desired result.

| **Exposure** | **Development** | **Common Name** |
| ------------ | ------------ | ------------ |
| Normal | Normal | Normal Processing |
| Overexposed | Normal | Overexposure |
| Underexposed | Normal | Underexposure |
| Overexposed | Reduced | Pull Processing |
| Underexposed | Increased | Push Processing |
| Overexposed | Increased | Overexposure + Increased Development |
| Underexposed | Reduced | Underexposure + Reduced Development |

## Overexposure

For example:
- Ilford HP5+ 400, rated at ISO 200. Then, developed normally.

Your negatives will result in more shadow detail with [denser negatives](/Terminology/Dense%20Negative.md).

## Underexposure

For example:
- Ilford HP5+ 400, rated at ISO 800. Then, developed normally.

Your negatives will result in reduced shadow detail and [thinner negatives](/Terminology/Thin%20Negative.md).

## Push Processing

For example:
- Kentmere Pan 400, shot at ISO 1600. Then, developed for longer.

Your negatives will result in an increase of contrast, increased grain, and reduced shadow detail.

## Pull Processing

For example:
- Ilford HP5+ 400, shot at ISO 200. Then, developed for less time.

Your negatives will result in lower contrast, but much better highlight retention.

## Overexposure + Increased Development

For example:
- Ilford HP5+ 400, rated at ISO 200. Then, developed for longer.

This will result in extremely [dense negatives](/Terminology/Dense%20Negative.md) with strong contrast, dense highlights, plentiful shadow detail, but a negative that will be difficult to print.

This combination is not recommended for most cases, and only used in very specific contrast manipulation methods.

## Underexposure + Decreased Development

For example:
- Ilford HP5+ 400, shot at ISO 800. Then, developed for less time.

This will result in extremely [thin negatives](/Terminology/Thin%20Negative.md) with less shadow detail and less highlight density.

This combination is not recommended for almost all cases.

## Misconceptions

For example:
- Ilford HP5+ 400, shot at 1600. Then, developed normally.

You are not "pushing" it. You're simply underexposing. Underexposing and overexposing only relate to what the photo was shot at relative to the box speed. This has nothing to do with the development of the film. Pushing and pulling is changing the [speed](/Terminology/Speed.md) you're shooting it *and* the development times.

Also, a pull does *not* cancel out an overexposure. A pull does not perfectly reverse an extra exposure because, as stated above, they are both independent variables. Extra exposure will primarily benefit your shadows, while reduced development times will primarily affect your highlights.
